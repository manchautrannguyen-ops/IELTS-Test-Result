Phần II — Writing

Nhận xét bài thi Writing

Task 2 essay analysis & band-by-band feedback

Task
Scientists agree that many people eat too much junk food and it is damaging their health. Some people think that this problem can be solved by education, while others believe that this education will not work. Discuss both views and give your opinion.

Essay

In our modern world, the consumption of junk food has increased, leading to various health problems. While some people believe that education can control this problem, others argue that education alone is not enough to change the eating habits. This essay will discuss both perspectives and explain why I believe the combination of educating and government's action is necessary.

Firstly, some people argue that awareness is the first step leading to a healthier lifestyle. When school teach children about nutrition and the dangers of fast food, students can develop better habits from a very young age. For example, learning about the value of prefer choosing fruit or healthy products instead of health damageable food. With adults, public health awareness campaigns or advertisements on television or social media can remind them of the long-term risks like heart disease, cancer or obesity - which has affects on responsible food choices.

On the other hand, many people believe that education is ineffective because of the public's need and the price of junk food. Fast food is often much cheaper and faster to buy or prepare than healthy meals, making it the popular choice for the public. Furthermore, junk food is designed to be additive, so although people may know that it is not good for their health, they still find junk food as the easiest and most convenience choice.

In my opinion, although education is important, it needs to be supported by the government. Rules should be more stricter or the government should consider increasing taxes on unhealthy products or limiting the advertising of junk food to children. If healthy food becomes more affordable, the lessons learned through education will be much more effective.

In conclusion, education plays an important role in forming the public, but it cannot solve the problem alone due to the low cost and convenience of processed food. That is why we need the participation of the government to encourage healthier food choices.

Detailed feedback (English)

Criterion Score Comment
Task Response 6 + Both views are addressed and a personal opinion is offered. The essay answers all parts of the prompt. + The position (education + government action) is clear and maintained throughout. - The "education works" side is thin. The only example given (children choosing fruit) is vague and not developed. A Band 7 essay would use concrete evidence or elaborated reasoning. - The conclusion introduces "processed food" without previous mention, and "forming the public" appears to be a mistranslation of "informing." These undermine the response.
Coherence & Cohesion 6 + Clear paragraph structure with an introduction, two body paragraphs, and a conclusion. Signposting words (Firstly, On the other hand, In my opinion) are used correctly. - "This essay will discuss both perspectives" is a mechanical phrase that adds no value. Avoid metadiscourse like this at Band 7+. - Cohesion within paragraphs is sometimes mechanical. Ideas in paragraph 2 (children → adults) feel listed rather than logically connected.
Lexical resource 5-6 + Some topic vocabulary is used: "nutrition," "obesity," "heart disease," "public health awareness campaigns." - "health damageable food": not natural English. Say "harmful food" or "food that is bad for health." - "additive" should be "addictive." A single-letter error changes the meaning entirely. - "most convenience choice": should be "most convenient." Adjective/noun confusion. - "forming the public": likely means "informing." This is a significant lexical error in the conclusion. - Repetition of "junk food" throughout. Could paraphrase with "processed food," "fast food," "unhealthy food options."
Grammatical range & accuracy 5-6 + Sentences are generally understandable and do not prevent meaning from coming through. - "Rules should be more stricter": double comparative. Should be "stricter" or "more strict," not both. - "which has affects on": should be "which has an effect on" or "which affects." Noun/verb confusion. - "When school teach children": subject-verb agreement error. Should be "when schools teach." - Sentence variety is limited. Most sentences follow a simple subject-verb-object pattern. Using more subordinate clauses, conditionals, and passive structures would raise the band.
Overall estimated band 6.0

Detailed feedback (English)

Criterion Score Comment
Fluency & coherence 6 The candidate is willing to speak at length and generally maintains communication across all three parts. Part 1 opens well with a coherent, extended answer about hobbies, and Part 2 is the strongest section — the film description follows a logical sequence with clear progression from plot summary to personal motivation. However, fluency breaks down noticeably when the candidate attempts more complex ideas, particularly in the second half of Part 1 ("the song which suitable my vibe") and throughout Part 3, where repeated self-interruption and unresolved repairs ("that make uh that make the success the successful of the movie") suggest the candidate is struggling to match linguistic output to intended meaning. Discourse markers and connectives are present ("however," "also," "besides that") but their use is not always controlled under pressure. This pattern — competent at familiar/narrative tasks, unstable at abstract ones — is characteristic of Band 6.
Lexical resource 6 The candidate demonstrates a genuine range of vocabulary, with notably strong choices in Part 1 ("history buff," "diving into old chronicles," "classical literature," "night owl") and appropriate register in Part 2 ("materialistic," "primary heir"). These moments suggest a lexical resource that can reach Band 7 in comfortable territory. However, precision and consistency fall away under pressure: "big kids in Vietnam" is a non-standard collocation likely transferred from Vietnamese, "the successful of the movie" shows word form confusion under self-correction, and some phrasing in Part 3 is repetitive and imprecise. The candidate does not consistently paraphrase — rather, they tend to repeat the same word when searching for an alternative. Overall, the range is adequate and meaning is generally clear, but the unevenness across parts limits the score to Band 6.
Grammatical range & accuracy 5-6 The candidate attempts a variety of structures, including relative clauses, concessive constructions, and reported speech, which demonstrates awareness of complexity. However, errors are frequent and sometimes severe enough to obscure meaning. Key recurring problems include missing third-person -s ("he finally understand"), incorrect past participle ("has already gave"), tense inconsistency within narrative passages (mixing simple past and present perfect without clear logic), and complete clause collapse under cognitive load ("even the best actors or actress can it came through fully make a movie"). Simple sentences within the response are generally accurate, but the greater accuracy at simple sentence level compared to complex structures is a defining Band 5–6 feature. The frequency and range of errors across the response place this criterion at the lower end of Band 6, pulling toward 5.5.
Pronunciation 6 The candidate's speech is generally intelligible throughout and does not cause significant communication breakdown. Phonological control is sufficient for a listener to follow the message without undue effort. However, there are a few notable mispronunciations of target vocabulary — "night owl," "heir," and "compelling" — which is a recurring pattern: the candidate selects sophisticated words but has not secured their phonological form. This gap between lexical ambition and phonological accuracy is a Band 6 characteristic. There is some natural connected speech in the more fluent passages of Part 2, but hesitation sounds (uh, uhm) are frequent and occasionally intrusive, particularly in Parts 1 and 3. With more consistent control of word-level pronunciation for less common items, this criterion has clear potential to reach Band 7.
Overall estimated band 6.0
